# Financial Terms Glossary

Plain English definitions of financial terms used in FinanceToolkit.

---

## A

**Assets**
Everything a company owns that has value. Includes cash, buildings, equipment, inventory, and money owed to them.

**Asset Turnover**
How many dollars of sales the company generates for each dollar of assets. Higher = more efficient.

**Altman Z-Score**
A bankruptcy prediction score combining 5 financial ratios. Above 2.99 = safe, below 1.81 = risky.

---

## B

**Balance Sheet**
A financial snapshot showing what a company owns (assets), owes (liabilities), and the difference (equity) at a specific point in time.

**Beta**
How much a stock moves compared to the overall market. Beta of 1 = moves with market. Beta of 2 = twice as volatile.

**Book Value**
What a company would be worth if it sold all assets and paid all debts. Also called "net assets" or "shareholders' equity."

---

## C

**COGS (Cost of Goods Sold)**
The direct costs of making the products a company sells. Includes materials, labor, and manufacturing overhead.

**Cash Flow**
Actual money moving in and out of a company. Different from profit because it's real cash, not accounting entries.

**Current Assets**
Assets that can be converted to cash within one year: cash, inventory, accounts receivable.

**Current Liabilities**
Debts due within one year: accounts payable, short-term loans, current portion of long-term debt.

**Current Ratio**
Current Assets / Current Liabilities. Measures ability to pay short-term bills. Above 1 = can pay bills.

---

## D

**Debt-to-Equity Ratio**
Total Debt / Total Equity. Measures how much the company relies on debt vs. shareholder money.

**Depreciation**
Spreading the cost of an asset over its useful life. A building bought for $1M might be depreciated over 30 years.

**Dividends**
Cash payments to shareholders from company profits.

**DuPont Analysis**
Breaking down ROE into three parts: profit margin, asset turnover, and leverage. Shows HOW a company achieves its ROE.

---

## E

**EBIT**
Earnings Before Interest and Taxes. Operating profit before financing costs and taxes.

**EBITDA**
Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. A measure of operating cash flow.

**Enterprise Value (EV)**
Market Cap + Total Debt - Cash. The theoretical takeover price of a company.

**Equity**
What shareholders own. Total Assets minus Total Liabilities. Also called "shareholders' equity" or "book value."

**EPS (Earnings Per Share)**
Net Income / Number of Shares. Profit per share of stock.

---

## F

**F-Score (Piotroski)**
A 0-9 score measuring financial health based on 9 criteria. 8-9 = strong, 0-4 = weak.

**Free Cash Flow**
Cash from operations minus capital expenditures. Money available to pay dividends, reduce debt, or invest.

---

## G

**Gross Margin**
(Revenue - COGS) / Revenue. Percentage of sales left after production costs.

**Gross Profit**
Revenue minus Cost of Goods Sold. Money left after paying for the product itself.

---

## I

**Income Statement**
Shows revenue, expenses, and profit over a period (quarter or year). Also called "P&L" (Profit and Loss).

**Interest Coverage Ratio**
EBIT / Interest Expense. How many times the company can pay its interest from operating profit.

**Intrinsic Value**
What a company is theoretically worth based on future cash flows. Used in DCF analysis.

---

## L

**Leverage**
Using debt to amplify returns. High leverage = more debt = more risk.

**Liabilities**
What a company owes: loans, accounts payable, bonds, leases.

**Liquidity**
How easily assets can be converted to cash. Cash is most liquid. Real estate is illiquid.

---

## M

**Market Cap (Market Capitalization)**
Stock Price Ã— Number of Shares Outstanding. Total market value of a company.

**Margin**
Usually refers to profit as a percentage of revenue. "10% margin" means 10 cents profit per dollar of sales.

**Maximum Drawdown**
Largest peak-to-trough decline in value. If a stock went from $100 to $60, maximum drawdown is -40%.

---

## N

**Net Income**
The "bottom line" - total profit after all expenses, interest, and taxes. Also called "earnings" or "profit."

**Net Margin**
Net Income / Revenue. Percentage of revenue that becomes profit.

---

## O

**Operating Income**
Profit from core business operations, before interest and taxes. Revenue minus operating expenses.

**Operating Cash Flow**
Cash generated from regular business operations. Different from net income because it's actual cash.

---

## P

**P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)**
Stock Price / EPS. How much investors pay for each dollar of earnings.

**P/B Ratio (Price-to-Book)**
Stock Price / Book Value per Share. How much investors pay relative to net assets.

**PEG Ratio**
P/E / Earnings Growth Rate. P/E adjusted for growth expectations.

---

## Q

**Quick Ratio**
(Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities. Stricter liquidity test than current ratio.

---

## R

**Revenue**
Total money from sales before any expenses. Also called "sales" or "top line."

**ROA (Return on Assets)**
Net Income / Total Assets. How efficiently the company uses assets to generate profit.

**ROE (Return on Equity)**
Net Income / Shareholders' Equity. Return generated on shareholder investment.

**ROIC (Return on Invested Capital)**
Return on all capital invested in the company (debt + equity).

**Risk-Free Rate**
Return on "zero risk" investments, typically US Treasury bonds. Used as baseline for measuring excess returns.

---

## S

**Sharpe Ratio**
(Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Standard Deviation. Return earned per unit of risk.

**Solvency**
Ability to pay long-term debts. A solvent company can meet all obligations.

**Standard Deviation**
Measure of volatility - how much returns vary from the average. Higher = more volatile.

---

## T

**Total Assets**
Everything the company owns: cash, receivables, inventory, property, equipment, intangibles.

**Total Debt**
All interest-bearing obligations: loans, bonds, notes payable.

**Treasury Rate**
Interest rate on US government bonds. The 10-year Treasury rate is commonly used as a benchmark.

---

## V

**Valuation**
Determining what a company or stock is worth. Methods include P/E, DCF, and comparable analysis.

**Value at Risk (VaR)**
Maximum expected loss at a given confidence level. "95% VaR of -3%" means 95% of days you won't lose more than 3%.

**Volatility**
How much a stock price moves up and down. High volatility = large price swings.

---

## W

**WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital)**
The average cost of all capital (debt + equity). Used as a discount rate in DCF analysis.

**Working Capital**
Current Assets - Current Liabilities. Money available for day-to-day operations.

---

## Z

**Z-Score**
See Altman Z-Score. Bankruptcy prediction model.
